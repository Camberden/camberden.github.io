	This recent Supreme Court case, orally argued on December 8, 2025, arose from President Trump's having fired two Federal Trade Commissioners, Alvaro Bedoya and Rebecca Slaughter, and its question in focus is: “Should the president have the ability to remove independent regulatory agency leadership for any reason?” This case bears importance in that it it may reshape the nature of the checks and balances of the executive branch over independent regulatory agencies such as the Federal Trade Commission and the Federal Reserve. The prevailing judicial precedent concerning presidential power to remove leadership of the Federal Trade Commission stems from the 1935 case law, Humphrey’s Executor v. United States where the Court held that the Constitution does not bestow illimitable power of removal to the president over these agencies’ leadership but rather a limited power of removal only for causes of “incapacity, neglect of duty, malfeasance in office.” It also holds that the Federal Trade Commission is an entity established through Article I of the Constitution and enabled to rulemake through Congress thus granting the agency a quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial quality that insulates it from a president’s complete control (Howe, 2025).

      A ruling in favor of the petitioner in this case would in essence overturn Humphrey’s Executor v. United States and restore presidential power over independent regulatory agencies as if through the lens of the former judicial precedent established in 1926 through Myers v. United States where the Court upheld the president’s right to remove officers who are units of an executive department. A ruling in favor of the respondent would uphold the prevailing multi-branch control structure of agency leadership that Justice Elena Kagan calls the longstanding “bargain” in which agencies are delegated legislative power in exchange for congressional oversight (Smarick, 2026).

  

      This case is related to our course content in that it directly involves the balance of power among the branches of government, particularly between executive and legislative. As for its connection to business law, the reshaping of this balance of power would differently determine the policy outcomes of independent regulatory agency actions that would apply to business operation and the economic environments in which they operate. Consider that, through selective removal and appointment, a president’s opinion on internet policy or interest rates could be officiated despite initial agency disagreement.

  

      This issue may impact my local community in a broad sense: following presidential inaugurations, we may see immediate overhauls to regulatory agency leadership whose policy agendas may change social, technological, and economic expectations. In particular, I am concerned with how this case might affect the Federal Reserve and the Federal Open Market Committee because potential interest rate hikes or declines may have political ramifications which could compel presidents to replace Reserve leadership with those who conform to politically expedient rate-setting which may diverge from what otherwise could be considered theoretically wise economic policy (Hyatt, 2025). This could lead to longer-term effects such as dramatic fluctuations in market conditions which could drive consumer and business activity trends artificially, local and beyond. A president may stand to be perceived more favorably in the short-term by lowering interest rates despite adverse option; one could attribute President Lyndon Johnson’s dissatisfaction with the Federal Reserve’s independence when, in 1965, the Reserve acted to raise interest rates against the spirit of the “Great Society” tax cuts (Fessenden, 2016). Were this bill to pass, a president would have an avenue to prevent the Reserve’s independent corrective action – were this law during President Johnson’s time, he would have had recourse to halter the rate-hikes for which the Federal Reserve leadership argued. Overall, though the passage of this bill may result in more immediate interest rate corrections or maintenance to tackle contemporary issues, the conflict of interest inherent in this power may give way to warned deleterious effects.

      For the summer 2026 ruling on this case, I believe that the Supreme Court should maintain a position that these agencies have a quasi-legislative nature whether or not Humphrey’s Executor v. United States is overturned. Congressional enablement for agencies to create rules through interpretation of broad statutory law ensures that Congress can better focus on general legislation of national importance rather than particular, domain-specific legislation. Precedent in the direction of further presidential control may serve to couple future policy of agencies with an implicit executive check, possibly requiring Congress at-large to begin performing domain-specific legislation which I believe would be less efficient and less effective use of government.
